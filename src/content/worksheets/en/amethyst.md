faq:
  - q: How does amethyst form?
    a: Amethyst forms inside bubbles and cracks in volcanic rock. Silica-rich hot water flows through these cavities and quartz crystallizes layer by layer. Trace iron atoms, irradiated by the Earth itself, give amethyst its purple color.
  - q: What is the Mohs hardness of amethyst?
    a: Amethyst is a 7 on the Mohs scale, same as ordinary quartz — harder than glass, softer than corundum (ruby/sapphire). A fingernail or a key cannot scratch it.
  - q: Where is most amethyst mined?
    a: Brazil, Uruguay, Zambia, and Madagascar are the major sources. Brazil's Minas Gerais state is especially famous for large amethyst geodes.
  - q: Why does amethyst change color when heated?
    a: Heating changes the state of the iron ions in the crystal lattice, turning amethyst yellow or orange. Most "citrine" sold on the market is actually heat-treated amethyst.
  - q: Is amethyst a good starter crystal for kids to collect?
    a: Yes. Amethyst is affordable, widely available, durable, and beautiful — a perfect first specimen for a young rock collection.

## What's inside this worksheet?

The 5-page amethyst worksheet takes your child from deep inside the Earth all the way to the gem in a jewelry box:

1. **Where does amethyst live?** Inside volcanic rock bubbles, crystal by crystal.
2. **Why purple?** A mix of iron atoms and natural radiation.
3. **Mohs hardness chart**: amethyst is a 7 — harder than a coin, softer than a ruby.
4. **World map of sources**: Brazil, Uruguay, Madagascar.
5. **My collector's card**: cut it out and start a rock passport.

## Why start a mineral series with amethyst?

Because its story is the easiest to "see". You can tell your child: *"This purple was painted by the Earth over a million years."* Then hand them a geode slice — they will get it.
